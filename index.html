Our mission

Providing care, comfort, and a path forward for animals in need.

A Fund for Veterinary Care is a nonprofit organization dedicated to helping stray and abandoned animals receive essential medical and surgical care.

We believe every animal deserves the chance to be treated with dignity, compassion, and skilled veterinary attention—especially those who are vulnerable, rescued, or without a permanent home.

About Dr. Dhillon

Five decades of veterinary experience, rooted in service.

Dr. Dhillon has cared for animals as a veterinarian for more than 50 years. For over 30 of those years, he has worked closely with local rescue groups and spay/neuter clinics to help subsidize medical care for animals in need.

His work reflects a lifelong commitment to animal welfare, practical community support, and compassionate veterinary medicine. Dr. Dhillon is also an award-winning community entrepreneur recognized by the State of Maryland.

For him, this mission is personal. Over the years, he has welcomed more than half a dozen rescue animals into his own family, bringing the same care and commitment into his home that he brings to the animals he serves.

Pet care education

Basic care tips for dogs and cats

Simple preventive care can help pets stay healthier, more comfortable, and better protected.

Preventive care

Schedule regular wellness visits so a veterinarian can monitor weight, skin, teeth, heart, mobility, and early signs of illness.

Vaccinations

Keep pets current on core vaccines and ask your veterinarian which lifestyle-based vaccines are recommended for your pet.

Dental health

Bad breath, drooling, difficulty chewing, or red gums can be signs of dental disease. Routine dental care can reduce pain and infection risk.

Nutrition

Feed a balanced diet appropriate for your pet’s age, size, and health needs. Avoid sudden diet changes unless guided by a veterinarian.

Parasite prevention

Use veterinarian-recommended flea, tick, heartworm, and intestinal parasite prevention based on your pet’s risk and local environment.

When to seek veterinary care

Contact a veterinarian promptly for trouble breathing, repeated vomiting, collapse, seizures, severe pain, wounds, inability to urinate, sudden weakness, or not eating.
